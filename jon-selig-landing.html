The Real Diagnosis

The Problems Haven't Changed.
They've Just Gotten Louder.

Sales leaders across industries are facing the same compounding failures. Here's what they're actually saying — in their own words.

01
Reps Lead with Product, Not Curiosity
Sales reps think products sell themselves. They open with features and skip discovery entirely. BDRs can't get their heads around the fact that they don't need to talk about the product at all in early conversations.
02
Discovery Is Broken
Poor discovery is epidemic. Reps are pitching too much and asking too little — and when they do ask, the questions are surface-level or scripted. Prospects drop nuggets of gold in discussions, but reps are too busy checking boxes to notice.
03
No Empathy for the Buyer
Sellers struggle to deeply understand their personas — not just what buyers do, but why they do it, what keeps them up at night, and what actually matters to their business. Without empathy, every conversation feels transactional.
04
Human Connection Has Atrophied
As tools have taken over, the science of conversations has been lost. Give-and-get exchanges feel transactional. Reps can't hold a room, build rapport, or navigate the emotional terrain of a deal. Meetings need to go better — full stop.
05
Cold Outreach Is Broken
Changing sales math means volume no longer works. Spam filters, dead phone lines, and ignored sequences are wasting budget. Organizations are spending on outbound infrastructure while ignoring the message quality inside it.
06
Weak Business Acumen & Financial Fluency
Reps can't explain why an outcome matters in business terms. They lack the financial acumen to connect value to measurable results — a critical failure in an era of increased spending scrutiny and more demanding economic buyers.
07
Managers Coach Reactively, Not Deliberately
Most coaching is ad hoc or "when asked." Managers don't know how to coach for behavior change — and often don't have the time or tools to do it systematically. The same skills that make a great rep make a great coach, and most managers lack both.
08
Institutional Knowledge Never Reaches the Sales Floor
The company's best insights, customer stories, and competitive intelligence stay locked in the heads of founders and executives. New reps inherit nothing — and onboarding fails to transfer the narrative and domain fluency they desperately need.
09
Can't Navigate to Power or Mobilize Buyers
Reps get stuck in the wrong rooms. They can't navigate to economic buyers, build internal coalitions, or motivate stakeholders to act. "Bias to inaction" from buyers kills deals that should close.
10
Training Is One-and-Done — and Forgotten by Friday
SKO sessions fire people up for 48 hours. Then it's back to the same habits. Without spaced reinforcement and behavior-change design, even the best training content dissolves. Investment is wasted. Nothing sticks.
11
Active Listening Is Practically Extinct
Reps are planning their next sentence while the buyer is still talking. Poor active listening kills discovery, damages relationships, and signals to prospects that they're just another name in a sequence.
12
Methodology Has Been Deployed — Results Haven't Followed
You've invested in Sandler, ValueSelling, MEDDIC, or all three. And yet. The problem isn't the methodology — it's that reps lack the communication skills, storytelling instincts, and domain fluency to apply any framework well.
"They have 3.8× pipeline coverage and still can't close. The problem isn't pipeline. It's what's being said inside it."
Cost of inaction — revenue
Deals stall, discount-to-close becomes the default, and win rates quietly erode quarter after quarter.
Cost of inaction — talent
B-players plateau. A-players leave when they stop growing. Hiring cycles repeat because root causes are never addressed.
Cost of inaction — market
As AI commoditizes outreach, the human conversation becomes the only edge left — and most teams are under-investing in it precisely when it matters most.
